Personal Statement

I realized I could feel the same silence in completely different ways. The first time, I didn't understand it. The second time, I did. That difference changed the way I see myself in social situations and how I react to them now.

At the beginning of high school, I was genuinely excited. I had felt lonely before so I saw it as a fresh start. I expected to find close friends, to feel understood and to finally have the kind of social life I had imagined. During the first semester, I did meet people and we spent time together, but something felt off. Our interests didn't match much and the environment felt heavy. Conversations didn't excite me and instead of feeling connected, I felt distant.

Without realizing it, I started going quiet, not because I had nothing to say but because I didn't feel like I belonged in those conversations. At that time, I didn't question it. I assumed something was wrong with me. I thought maybe I just wasn't social enough or interesting enough to fit in. So I stayed silent. It felt easier than forcing myself into conversations that didn't feel natural or pretending to be someone I was not.

Months later, I found myself at a party, surrounded by loud music and people talking over each other. I tried to join in, but my words got lost and eventually I stopped trying. For a moment, I felt that same silence again, the same discomfort. My mind immediately told me the same thing: they are not listening because they are not interested in you.

But this time, I paused. Instead of listening to that thought, I looked around. People were interrupting each other, laughing loudly and barely paying full attention to anyone. The environment itself made it hard to be heard. In that moment, I realized something important: my mind was repeating a story that wasn't necessarily true.

The silence I felt wasn't always rejection. Sometimes, it was just noise, timing or my own hesitation. That realization became a turning point for me. I started paying less attention to what my mind assumed and more attention to what people were actually doing.

Most of the time, their actions didn't match the negative ideas in my head. They weren't rejecting me, they were simply distracted, busy or unaware.

Changing this hasn't been immediate. I still overthink and there are moments when staying quiet feels safer. But now I question those thoughts. I push myself to speak even if it's something small. I stay present instead of withdrawing and I remind myself that not every silence has the same meaning.

I've learned that my mind can work against me but it can also help me grow if I choose how to respond to it, and how to act. I'm still becoming more social but now I understand that silence doesn't define me, my actions do and that gives me confidence. This perspective continues shaping how I interact with others daily.
